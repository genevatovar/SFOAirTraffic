SFO Through Crisis:
A Passenger Story

When the world stops moving, airports feel it first.

25 Years of Disruption

SFO is one of the most recognized airports in the United States, known for its museum exhibits, dining, and sensory rooms. But behind that reputation is a passenger traffic story shaped by forces far outside the airport's control. This project traces over 25 years of monthly enplaned passenger data, from the aftermath of 9/11 and the 2008 recession to the near-total collapse of air travel during COVID-19 and the slow climb back. Through four visualizations, it explores how deep each disruption cut, whether domestic and international routes recovered at the same pace, which airlines grew or pulled back, and what seasonal patterns look like when you zoom out across two and a half decades.

01 — Overview

Seeing the Full Picture

Each cell is one month at SFO. The darker the cell, the more passengers flew that month. Summer peaks repeat like clockwork every year until COVID-19 nearly wipes out an entire row in 2020. No single event before it came close to the same scale of disruption.

02 — Global

Where SFO Connects

Asia and Europe anchor SFO's international reach. Toggle between eras to watch those connections shrink during the crisis and slowly rebuild through recovery. Some routes that existed before 2020 have not returned at all.

03 — Recovery

Domestic vs. International

Domestic routes bounced back faster. International traffic lagged significantly — travel bans, closed borders, and reduced demand kept those routes suppressed well into 2022. The gap between the two tells a story of uneven recovery across two very different passenger markets.

04 — Airlines

Who Grew, Who Pulled Back

United dominates SFO throughout, but the pandemic reshuffled the carriers below it. Some airlines never returned to their pre-COVID volume. Others used the disruption as an opportunity — cutting costs, renegotiating gates, and emerging with a larger share of a smaller market.
